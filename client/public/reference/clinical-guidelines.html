1. Introduction

This guideline outlines the standard approach to prescribing and managing hormone therapy, metabolic therapies, and related wellness treatments at REM Health Medical Group, P.A. Treatments addressed include testosterone replacement therapy (TRT), female hormone therapy, peptide therapies, and medical weight management, including GLP-1 receptor agonists and related metabolic medications.

The purpose of this guideline is to ensure safe, effective, and evidence-informed care while maintaining consistent clinical standards across all providers. These guidelines are intended to support clinical decision-making and do not replace provider judgment.

Providers may deviate from these guidelines when clinically appropriate based on individual patient needs. Any significant deviation should be clearly documented in the medical record and may require review or approval by the Chief Medical Officer.

This practice operates within a telemedicine model; certain conditions may require referral for in-person evaluation.

All healthcare professionals involved in prescribing or managing these therapies are expected to follow these guidelines to ensure consistency, patient safety, and regulatory compliance.

2. Indications for Treatment

Hormone therapy may be considered in patients based on a combination of clinical symptoms and laboratory findings, with emphasis on individualized care.

Men

  • Symptoms consistent with testosterone deficiency, including fatigue, reduced libido, erectile dysfunction, depressed mood, reduced muscle mass, or increased body fat

  • Suboptimal testosterone levels in conjunction with clinical symptoms

  • Diagnosed hypogonadism or conditions affecting endogenous testosterone production

  • Persistent symptoms of androgen deficiency despite testosterone levels within conventional laboratory reference ranges

Women

  • Symptoms associated with perimenopause or menopause, including hot flashes, night sweats, sleep disturbance, reduced libido, vaginal dryness, fatigue, mood changes, or changes in body composition

  • Clinical or laboratory evidence of hormonal imbalance contributing to symptoms

Peptide or Metabolic Therapy

May be considered in patients with age-related decline in recovery or performance, reduced lean muscle mass, impaired recovery from exercise, metabolic dysfunction or insulin resistance, or persistent fatigue despite optimization of lifestyle factors.

Medical Weight Management

May be considered in patients with BMI ≥25, or BMI <25 for maintenance following medically supervised weight loss, or presence of obesity-related comorbidities including type 2 diabetes or prediabetes, hypertension, dyslipidemia, obstructive sleep apnea, cardiovascular disease, osteoarthritis or joint pain, and nonalcoholic fatty liver disease.

GLP-1 receptor agonists and related medications should be used as part of a comprehensive treatment plan that includes nutrition, physical activity, and lifestyle modification.

3. Hormone Therapy Contraindications and Risk Stratification

Hormone therapy should only be initiated after careful evaluation of patient-specific risk factors. Risk assessment should include review of thrombotic history, cardiovascular risk, sleep apnea status, hematologic parameters, and fertility goals.

Absolute Contraindications

Hormone therapy should not be initiated in the presence of:

  • Active or untreated hormone-sensitive malignancy (e.g., prostate cancer, breast cancer)

  • Active or recent thromboembolic event (DVT, pulmonary embolism, ischemic stroke) within the past 12 months

  • Known hypercoagulable state with active or recent thrombotic event

  • Severe uncontrolled cardiovascular disease (e.g., unstable angina, recent myocardial infarction)

  • Severe hepatic dysfunction

  • Pregnancy

  • Unexplained vaginal bleeding (in females)

Relative Contraindications and Use with Caution

History of Thromboembolic Disease

  • Prior DVT or pulmonary embolism >12 months ago without recurrence

  • Patients currently on stable anticoagulation (e.g., warfarin or DOAC therapy)

  • History of provoked clot (e.g., surgery, immobility) with resolved risk factors

Considerations: assess provoking vs. unprovoked event; confirm stability on anticoagulation; consider hematology coordination; avoid supraphysiologic dosing; monitor hematocrit closely.

Known or Suspected Hypercoagulable Disorders

  • Factor V Leiden

  • Prothrombin gene mutation

  • Protein C or Protein S deficiency

  • Antithrombin deficiency

TRT is not recommended in patients with prior unprovoked thrombotic event, recurrent events, or recent event (<12 months). May be considered with caution in asymptomatic patients without prior thrombotic events, or clearly provoked remote events (>12 months) on a case-by-case basis.

ℹ️ Management Approach

• Perform individualized risk assessment prior to initiation

• Utilize shared decision-making; document risk-benefit discussion

• Consider hematology consultation when appropriate

• Initiate conservative dosing; avoid supraphysiologic testosterone levels

• Monitor hematocrit closely; follow erythrocytosis management guidelines

Patients should be educated on signs of thromboembolic events: leg swelling or pain, chest pain or shortness of breath, neurologic deficits.

Fertility Considerations

  • Desire for current or future fertility / actively attempting conception

Exogenous testosterone suppresses the HPG axis and may significantly reduce or eliminate sperm production. TRT is not recommended as first-line therapy in patients actively attempting conception. Alternatives include hCG and enclomiphene. TRT may be considered only after thorough counseling and documentation of patient understanding of potential spermatogenesis suppression.

Clinical Notes

  • Testosterone suppression of spermatogenesis may occur even at physiologic TRT doses

  • Recovery of spermatogenesis after TRT discontinuation is variable and may take months to over a year

  • Concurrent hCG may help preserve intratesticular testosterone but does not guarantee fertility preservation

  • Providers should document discussion of fertility impact prior to initiating testosterone therapy

  • Consider baseline semen analysis if fertility preservation is a stated patient goal

  • Refer to a fertility specialist when clinically indicated

Other Relative Contraindications

  • Polycythemia / elevated hematocrit

  • Untreated or poorly controlled obstructive sleep apnea

  • Controlled hypertension, stable coronary artery disease, or metabolic syndrome

  • Mild-to-moderate hepatic or renal impairment

GLP-1 and Metabolic Therapy Contraindications

Should not be initiated in patients with: pregnancy, breastfeeding, hypersensitivity to GLP-1 medications, personal or family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma, MEN2, severe gastrointestinal disease affecting gastric emptying, history of pancreatitis, or diabetic ketoacidosis.

Phentermine Contraindications

Should not be initiated in patients with: hypersensitivity, uncontrolled hypertension or arrhythmias, cardiovascular disease, hyperthyroidism, glaucoma, severe anxiety or agitated states, pregnancy, breastfeeding, history of substance abuse, or MAOI use within 14 days.

MALE TESTOSTERONE THERAPY PROTOCOL

Overview and Treatment Goals

Testosterone Replacement Therapy (TRT) is used to treat men with symptoms of testosterone deficiency and laboratory evidence of reduced testosterone levels. Diagnosis should be based on consistent clinical symptoms in combination with laboratory evaluation. The goal of testosterone therapy at REM Health Medical Group is to restore physiologic androgen activity and improve symptoms while maintaining appropriate safety monitoring.

The typical target range for total testosterone during therapy is approximately 800-1200 ng/dL, provided patients demonstrate clinical benefit and maintain appropriate safety parameters. Treatment decisions are guided by both clinical response and laboratory values, with emphasis on symptom improvement, overall well-being, and functional health outcomes.

ℹ️ Free Testosterone Note

Testosterone therapy should not be determined solely by total testosterone. A portion of circulating testosterone is bound to SHBG and albumin, leaving only free testosterone as the physiologically active form.

Patients with elevated SHBG may have adequate total testosterone but insufficient free testosterone, resulting in persistent symptoms. Clinical decision-making should incorporate patient symptoms, total testosterone, free testosterone, and SHBG when appropriate.

Testosterone Cypionate (200 mg/mL)

Indications

  • Fatigue

  • Decreased libido

  • Erectile dysfunction

  • Decreased muscle mass

  • Increased body fat

  • Depressed mood

  • Poor exercise recovery

  • Cognitive decline

Routine laboratory monitoring: every 6 months when requested by the patient or clinically indicated; annually when required per protocol.

Dosing

Starting dose: 120 mg weekly IM or SC for patients new to TRT. Provider may match previous stable dosing when transferring care. May be administered once weekly or split twice weekly. Starting dose may be reduced at provider discretion based on comorbid conditions or clinical judgment.

Lab draw timing: day 4-6 after injection (once weekly), or the day before injection (twice weekly). For more frequent plans, draw before injection on the day it is due.

Dose Escalation Ladder: 20 mg increments every 6-8 weeks

Doses above 200 mg/week require evaluation for elevated SHBG, obesity, sleep apnea, alcohol use, medication interactions, or non-compliance.

Dose Escalation Safety Parameters

Escalation may occur only if total testosterone is below target range with persistent hypogonadal symptoms AND all of the following are within limits:

Parameter Maximum Acceptable Value
RBC < 6.0 x10⁶/uL
Hemoglobin < 18 g/dL
Hematocrit < 56%
Estradiol < 60 pg/mL

Target hormone levels: Total Testosterone 800-1200 ng/dL | Free Testosterone 20-25 ng/dL (200-300 pg/mL). Free testosterone may exceed 300 pg/mL in patients on exogenous testosterone, particularly with reduced SHBG. Mild-to-moderate elevations do not require automatic dose reduction if total testosterone is within range, patient is asymptomatic, and safety markers are acceptable. Symptom resolution remains the primary clinical endpoint.

Kyzatrex (Oral Testosterone Undecanoate)

Kyzatrex is an oral testosterone undecanoate formulation absorbed through the intestinal lymphatic system, avoiding first-pass hepatic metabolism. Because absorption is fat-dependent, dosing must occur with meals containing adequate dietary fat.

Indications: symptomatic hypogonadism; patients preferring oral therapy or unable to use injectable. Initial dosing: 200 mg orally twice daily approximately 10-12 hours apart with fat-containing meals (eggs, avocado, olive oil, nuts, fatty fish, full-fat dairy). Low-fat meals may significantly reduce absorption and lead to subtherapeutic levels.

FEMALE HORMONE THERAPY PROTOCOLS

Female Testosterone Cypionate (25 mg/mL)

Testosterone therapy may be considered in select female patients to support libido, energy, body composition, and overall well-being. Therapy should be individualized and maintained within physiologic female ranges to minimize the risk of androgenic side effects.

Indications: Low libido, fatigue, reduced muscle mass, poor recovery from exercise, menopausal symptoms

Lab Schedule

When Labs Required
Baseline CBC, CMP, Total Testosterone, Progesterone, Estradiol, Lipid Panel
6-8 Weeks CBC, CMP, Total Testosterone, Progesterone, Estradiol
Every 6 Months CBC, CMP, Total Testosterone, Progesterone, Estradiol
Annual CBC, CMP, Total Testosterone, Progesterone, Estradiol, Lipid Panel

Starting dose: 5 mg once weekly or split twice weekly IM or SC. Typical range: 5-10 mg weekly. Target testosterone: 30-80 ng/dL. Adjust every 6-8 weeks based on symptoms and levels. Monitor for: acne, hair growth, voice changes, hair loss.

Estradiol (E2) Transdermal

Estradiol (E2) is the primary and most biologically active estrogen in females. Transdermal allows steady absorption while avoiding first-pass hepatic metabolism, making it the preferred option in hormone optimization protocols.

Indications

  • Menopausal or perimenopausal symptoms

  • Vasomotor symptoms (hot flashes, night sweats)

  • Vaginal dryness or genitourinary symptoms

  • Low libido associated with estrogen deficiency

  • Mood instability or cognitive changes

  • Poor sleep quality

  • Joint pain or decreased recovery

  • Low serum estradiol with clinical symptoms

Dosing

Standard starting dose: 0.5 mg applied topically once daily (inner thigh, wrist, or forearm). Typical clinical range: 0.5-1 mg daily. May titrate based on symptom response and serum estradiol levels. Rotate application sites; allow full absorption before contact with others. Dose adjustments no more frequently than every 6-8 weeks.

Target Estradiol Ranges (Females)

Range Level Clinical Association
Conservative / Traditional 20-40 pg/mL Typically sufficient for minimal symptom control
Optimized Clinical Range 40-80 pg/mL Improved mood, energy, sleep, libido, joint comfort
Upper-End Consideration 80-100 pg/mL Select patients with persistent symptoms, well-tolerated
Lower-End (Undertreated) <30 pg/mL Persistent vasomotor symptoms, poor sleep, low libido

⚠️ When NOT to Increase Estradiol

• Estradiol already within optimized range (40-80 pg/mL)

• Breast tenderness, fluid retention, irritability, headaches, or breakthrough bleeding present

• Suspected or confirmed progesterone deficiency

• Low testosterone as the primary driver of symptoms

• Early in therapy (<6-8 weeks) | Inconsistent application or poor absorption

• Elevated thrombotic or cardiovascular risk

Contraindications: Active or untreated estrogen-sensitive malignancy, unexplained vaginal bleeding, active or recent thromboembolic event (<12 months), known high-risk hypercoagulable state with prior thrombotic event, active liver disease (oral estradiol specific; does not apply to transdermal formulation)

Estradiol (E2) Oral

Oral estradiol undergoes first-pass hepatic metabolism, which can influence lipid metabolism, clotting factors, and inflammatory markers. While transdermal estrogen is generally preferred, oral estradiol may be utilized in select patients based on clinical context and patient preference.

Starting dose: 0.5 mg - 1 mg orally once daily. Typical range: 0.5-2 mg daily. Lower starting doses recommended in patients with increased cardiovascular risk. Gradual titration preferred.

⛔ Additional monitoring for oral estradiol

Oral estradiol undergoes first-pass hepatic metabolism, increasing hepatic production of clotting factors and triglycerides.

Higher thrombotic risk compared to transdermal. Preferred in lower-risk patients or those requiring oral administration.

• Monitor lipid panel and blood pressure in addition to standard hormone therapy labs

• Liver function tests (AST, ALT) as clinically indicated, particularly in patients with baseline hepatic risk factors.

• Active liver disease is an additional contraindication vs. transdermal

Use with caution in:

• Uncontrolled hypertension

• Hypertriglyceridemia (oral estradiol increases triglycerides via first-pass hepatic metabolism)

Progesterone

Progesterone plays an essential role in endometrial protection, sleep regulation, mood stability, and overall hormonal balance. Oral micronized progesterone is preferred due to its favorable safety profile and beneficial effects on sleep and central nervous system function.

Indications

  • Intact uterus receiving estrogen therapy (endometrial protection: required)

  • Sleep disturbance or insomnia

  • Anxiety or mood instability

  • Irritability or emotional lability

  • Perimenopausal hormonal imbalance

  • Symptoms of relative estrogen dominance

  • Irregular menstrual cycles (perimenopausal patients)

Starting dose: 100 mg orally at bedtime. Typical range: 100-200 mg nightly. Bedtime dosing preferred due to sedative effects. Dose adjustments no more frequently than every 6-8 weeks.

Administer on an empty stomach or with light food for consistent absorption.

Target progesterone: ~5-15 ng/mL (when measured). Routine targeting of serum progesterone is not always necessary. Clinical response (sleep, mood, symptom control) should guide therapy.

Contraindications

Contraindications: active or untreated hormone-sensitive malignancy (unless cleared by appropriate specialist), unexplained vaginal bleeding, severe liver dysfunction, known hypersensitivity to progesterone formulations.

Use caution in patients with: history of hormone-sensitive cancers; significant depression or mood disorders (monitor closely); cardiovascular disease.

Estradiol : Progesterone Balance

Estradiol and progesterone function as a complementary hormonal system. Imbalance, particularly relative estrogen excess without adequate progesterone, can lead to persistent symptoms despite otherwise "normal" laboratory values.

Pattern Common Symptoms Clinical Approach
Balanced Hormone State Symptoms well controlled Maintain current regimen
Relative Estrogen Dominance Breast tenderness, fluid retention, irritability, poor sleep, breakthrough bleeding Optimize or increase progesterone; avoid reflexive estradiol escalation
True Estradiol Deficiency Hot flashes, vaginal dryness, low libido, poor sleep, low energy Increase estradiol cautiously; ensure progesterone supported if uterus present

ADJUNCTIVE ANABOLIC THERAPIES

Nandrolone Decanoate

Nandrolone decanoate is an anabolic agent that may be utilized as an adjunct to testosterone therapy in select male patients to support joint health, connective tissue recovery, and lean mass preservation. Nandrolone should only be prescribed in patients already on testosterone therapy. Contraindications: history of prostate cancer, significant polycythemia, severe dyslipidemia, untreated sleep apnea.

Dosing Ratio Guidance

Weekly Testosterone Dose Nandrolone Dose
Less than 150 mg/week 50 mg/week
150 mg/week or greater 100 mg/week

Maximum: 100 mg weekly. Doses above this range are not recommended. Monitoring: CBC, CMP, Total Testosterone, Estradiol, Lipid Panel at 6 months; annual: CBC, CMP, Total Testosterone, PSA (age 45+ or indicated), Estradiol, Lipid Panel. Monitor for erythrocytosis, lipid changes, fluid retention. Not recommended for female patients.

Oral Androgen Adjuncts: Oxandrolone and Stanozolol

These 17-alpha-alkylated oral androgens may be used as short-term adjuncts to testosterone therapy in carefully selected patients with persistent symptoms despite optimized TRT. Strict hepatic screening and monitoring are required.

Common Indications: sarcopenia with functional decline, poor lean mass response despite optimized TRT, catabolic states or prolonged recovery, severe musculoskeletal weakness impacting function

Oxandrolone

Relatively favorable anabolic-to-androgenic profile with less hepatic strain than many other oral anabolic agents. Still carries potential hepatic and lipid effects.

Contraindications: liver disease, elevated liver enzymes, pregnancy or breastfeeding, known hypersensitivity

Patient Typical Dose Max Dose / Duration
Male 25 mg daily (higher: 50 mg in select cases) 90-day max; mandatory 30-day break between courses
Female 5 mg daily 10 mg daily max; 90-day max course

ℹ️ Oxandrolone and Estrogen

Oxandrolone is DHT-derived and does not aromatize to estradiol. However, it commonly reduces SHBG, which may increase the free fraction of circulating sex hormones including estradiol.

Clinical management of estradiol should be based on symptoms and measured levels, not the presence of oxandrolone therapy alone.

Stanozolol

Higher anabolic activity with lower androgenic activity compared with testosterone. Carries increased risk of hepatotoxicity and lipid disruption.

Contraindications: liver disease, elevated liver enzymes, known hypersensitivity

Male dosing only: 25 mg daily maximum. Maximum course duration: 60 days. Mandatory 30-day break between courses. Repeat CMP and lipid panel required prior to subsequent course.

Monitoring: All Oral Androgens

When Labs Required
Required 6 Month (Androgen Therapy) CBC, CMP, Total Testosterone, Estradiol, Lipid Panel
Additional Oral Androgen Monitoring AST, ALT, Lipid Panel (HDL suppression, LDL elevation)

Acceptable baseline parameters: AST <100 IU/L, ALT <100 IU/L, total bilirubin <2 mg/dL, no evidence of active liver disease. Discontinue or adjust therapy if AST or ALT rise >2-3x upper limit of normal, bilirubin becomes elevated, or patient develops symptoms of liver dysfunction.

AROMATASE INHIBITOR AND GYNECOMASTIA MANAGEMENT

Estradiol in men is primarily produced through aromatization of testosterone. Absolute estradiol values alone do not determine clinical significance and must be interpreted in context of symptoms and total testosterone levels. Treatment decisions should prioritize symptom resolution and physiologic balance rather than targeting a specific estradiol value.

Core Clinical Principles

  • Do not treat estradiol levels in isolation

  • Base treatment decisions on symptoms and laboratory values together

  • Avoid prophylactic use of aromatase inhibitors

  • Address modifiable contributors prior to pharmacologic intervention

Modifiable contributors to aromatization: excessive testosterone dosing, obesity or increased body fat, alcohol intake, infrequent injection schedules. Increasing injection frequency or reducing testosterone dose may reduce aromatization and should be considered before pharmacologic intervention.

Estradiol-Testosterone Ratio (E2/TT)

Formula: E2/TT (%) = (Estradiol [pg/mL] / Total Testosterone [ng/dL]) x 100

Ratio Interpretation Clinical Approach
<3% Balanced aromatization No treatment
3-5% Borderline aromatization Treat symptoms, optimize TRT variables
>5% Disproportionate aromatization Consider intervention if symptomatic

Stepwise Management

Step Action
1 Elevated estradiol without symptoms: no treatment required; continue routine monitoring
2 Mild symptoms: increase injection frequency; reduce testosterone dose if appropriate; encourage fat loss
3 Moderate symptoms with elevated estradiol: anastrozole 0.25 mg orally once weekly (alt: exemestane 12.5 mg once weekly). Reassess in 6-8 weeks
4 Persistent symptoms: increase anastrozole to 0.25 mg twice weekly OR exemestane to 12.5 mg twice weekly
5 Gynecomastia: tamoxifen 10-20 mg orally once daily for 4-8 weeks (may extend to 12 weeks). May use with or without AI
6 Over-suppression: reduce or discontinue AI. If estradiol <15-20 pg/mL, discontinue AI immediately

Monitoring: recheck estradiol, total testosterone, CBC, CMP at 6-8 weeks after initiation or dose adjustment. Monitor for signs of estrogen over-suppression.

ERYTHROCYTOSIS AND ANEMIA MANAGEMENT

TRT-Associated Erythrocytosis

Hematocrit Level Interventions Follow-Up
<52% Continue TRT; monitor at routine intervals Routine interval

ℹ️ Clinical Notes

• Hematocrit elevation is dose-dependent and correlates with peak testosterone levels

• Increasing injection frequency is often effective prior to dose reduction

• Sleep apnea is a common and under-recognized contributor

• Dehydration and alcohol use may falsely elevate hematocrit

• TRT may be resumed once hematocrit returns to acceptable range and contributing factors have been addressed

TRT-Associated Anemia

Anemia in patients on testosterone therapy is uncommon but should prompt evaluation for underlying causes including iron deficiency, blood loss, or nutritional deficiency. Frequent blood donation or therapeutic phlebotomy may deplete iron stores over time.

Evaluation: ferritin, serum iron, TIBC, transferrin saturation, CBC (with trend review). If indicated: vitamin B12, folate, reticulocyte count.

Management: reduce or discontinue blood donation if applicable; treat confirmed iron deficiency with oral iron supplementation; review dietary intake. TRT may be continued if anemia is mild and patient is clinically stable. Hold TRT in cases of severe or unexplained anemia. Repeat CBC and iron studies in 6-8 weeks after intervention. Refer to primary care provider for moderate-to-severe or unexplained anemia.

ENDOGENOUS TESTOSTERONE SUPPORT AND FERTILITY PRESERVATION

Human Chorionic Gonadotropin (hCG)

hCG stimulates endogenous testosterone production through activation of LH receptors on Leydig cells. Used to preserve fertility, maintain testicular volume, or support endogenous hormone production, either as monotherapy or with TRT.

Contraindications: active or history of hormone-sensitive malignancy, uncontrolled thyroid or adrenal disease, symptomatic high estradiol (>60 pg/mL), known hypersensitivity.

Dosing: 250 IU twice weekly | 500 IU twice weekly | 1,000 IU three times weekly (fertility management). Higher dosing may be considered in fertility management if response to standard protocols is not effective.

When Labs Required
Baseline CBC, CMP, Total/Free Testosterone, Estradiol, LH, Lipid Panel, PSA (age 45+ or indicated)
Every 6 Months CBC, CMP, Total/Free Testosterone, Estradiol
Annual CBC, CMP, Total/Free Testosterone, Estradiol, Lipid Panel, LH, PSA (age 45+ or indicated)

Monitor for elevated estradiol, fluid retention, gynecomastia. Dose adjustment may be required if estradiol rises significantly.

Enclomiphene

Enclomiphene is a SERM that increases endogenous testosterone production by blocking estrogen-mediated negative feedback at the hypothalamus, resulting in increased LH and FSH secretion. Used as an alternative to exogenous testosterone for patients seeking to preserve fertility and maintain endogenous hormone production.

Contraindications: active or untreated hormone-sensitive malignancy, thromboembolic event within past 12 months, known hypercoagulable state, severe hepatic dysfunction, uncontrolled endocrine disorders, known hypersensitivity to SERMs.

Dosing: 12.5 mg three times weekly. If inadequate response: 25 mg three times weekly. Escalation options: every other day or daily dosing if needed.

Monitoring: baseline CBC, CMP, total testosterone, estradiol, LH. Follow-up every 6 months, then annually. Monitor for mood changes, visual disturbances, headaches.

Follicle Stimulating Hormone (FSH)

FSH directly stimulates spermatogenesis via action on Sertoli cells. Used in patients with impaired fertility, particularly secondary hypogonadism or inadequate response to hCG monotherapy. Typically used with hCG to optimize both testosterone production and sperm development.

Contraindications: primary testicular failure, active hormone-sensitive malignancy, known hypersensitivity to gonadotropins.

Dosing: 75 IU subcutaneous injection 2-3 times per week. Adjustments based on semen analysis (performed by external laboratory), clinical response, and fertility goals.

METABOLIC OPTIMIZATION AND WEIGHT MANAGEMENT

GLP-1 / GIP Metabolic Therapies

GLP-1 receptor agonists and related incretin-based therapies are used to support weight loss, glycemic control, and overall metabolic health. Therapies include semaglutide (GLP-1 receptor agonist) and tirzepatide (GLP-1/GIP dual agonist). These agents should be used as part of a comprehensive program including nutrition, exercise, and lifestyle modification.

Indications

  • BMI ≥25

  • BMI <25 for weight maintenance after prior medically supervised weight loss

  • Insulin resistance or prediabetes

  • Type 2 diabetes

  • Metabolic syndrome

Dosing Protocol

Semaglutide Weekly Dose
Step 1 Weeks 1-4: 0.25 mg
Step 2 Weeks 5-8: 0.5 mg
Step 3 Weeks 9-12: 1 mg
Step 4 Week 13+: 1.5-2 mg
Tirzepatide Titration (increase every 4 weeks as tolerated)
Titration 2.5 mg → 5 mg → 7.5 mg → 10 mg → 12.5 mg → 15 mg weekly

No baseline labs required prior to initiation. Required 3-month panel: CBC, CMP, TSH, Hemoglobin A1C, Lipid Panel. Every 6 months: same panel.

Contraindications: pregnancy or breastfeeding, personal or family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma, MEN2, history of pancreatitis, severe gastroparesis or GI motility disorder, hypersensitivity.

GLP-1 / GIP Microdosing Protocol

Semaglutide microdose: 0.25 mg weekly (may titrate to 0.25-0.5 mg weekly). Tirzepatide microdose: 2.5 mg weekly (may increase cautiously to 2.5-5 mg weekly). Indicated for BMI <25 weight maintenance, sensitivity to standard GLP dosing, mild appetite dysregulation, or conservative metabolic optimization.

Phentermine

Sympathomimetic anorectic agent for short-term weight management through central appetite suppression. Indications: BMI ≥30 or BMI ≥27 with obesity-related comorbidities.

Dosing: 37.5 mg orally once daily (may initiate at 18.75 mg in sensitive patients). Take in the morning; avoid late-day dosing (insomnia risk). Duration: typically 8-12 weeks.

Monitoring: baseline patient-reported BP and HR (if available); 3-month panel: CBC, CMP, TSH, Hemoglobin A1C, Lipid Panel; ongoing: blood pressure, heart rate, weight.

Contraindications: uncontrolled hypertension, cardiovascular disease or arrhythmias, hyperthyroidism, glaucoma, severe anxiety or agitated states, history of substance abuse, pregnancy or breastfeeding, MAOI use within 14 days.

Lipo B and L-Carnitine

Lipo B (lipotropic injection containing B vitamins and amino acids): 1 mL weekly SC or IM. Routine labs not required. Contraindications: hypersensitivity to components, severe liver disease (relative).

L-Carnitine (facilitates fatty acid transport into mitochondria): 500 mg (1 mL) IM twice weekly. Routine labs not required. Contraindications: hypersensitivity. Use caution in seizure disorders.

ERECTILE FUNCTION THERAPIES

Erectile dysfunction (ED) is multifactorial involving hormonal, vascular, neurologic, and psychological components. Phosphodiesterase type 5 (PDE-5) inhibitors are first-line therapy. Adjunctive therapies may be utilized in patients with suboptimal response.

Erectile Dysfunction Treatment Algorithm

Step Action
1 Optimize foundational factors: testosterone (total and free), estradiol balance, cardiovascular risk, metabolic health, psychological factors, medication contributors. If hypogonadism present, initiate or optimize TRT before escalating ED therapy.
2 First-line therapy (PDE-5 inhibitors): tadalafil (preferred for daily use / spontaneity) or sildenafil (preferred for situational use). Assess response. If adequate: continue. If inadequate: proceed to Step 3.
3 Optimization and adjunct: confirm proper administration; optimize testosterone and hormonal balance; consider switching PDE-5 inhibitor; add bremelanotide (PT-141) for central arousal/libido support; address psychogenic contributors.
4 Refractory ED: reassess for vascular, neurologic, or psychological causes; consider referral to urology; consider advanced therapies.
5 Ongoing management: reassess erectile function and satisfaction; adjust therapy based on response; monitor for complications; continue optimization of hormonal and metabolic factors.
Medication Dosing Key Notes
Tadalafil (daily) 2.5-5 mg once daily Can take with food; consistent timing
Tadalafil (as needed) 10-20 mg 30-60 min before (max once per 24 hrs)
Sildenafil 25-100 mg 30-60 min before (max once per 24 hrs) Take on empty stomach for best effect
Bremelanotide (PT-141) 1-2 mg SC 30-60 min before (max once per 24 hrs; max 8 doses/month) Inject abdomen or thigh; central arousal mechanism

⛔ Hard Stop: Contraindications for PDE-5 Inhibitors

• Concurrent nitrate use (e.g., nitroglycerin, isosorbide): life-threatening interaction

• Uncontrolled hypertension (for PT-141)

All treatment plans should be evaluated at regular intervals and reassessed at least annually, or more frequently based on clinical need. Adverse Events and Emergency Response

In the event of a suspected adverse reaction or medical emergency: instruct the patient to seek immediate emergency care (call 911) for severe or life-threatening symptoms; promptly assess the patient and discontinue therapy if clinically indicated; document all adverse events thoroughly in the medical record; complete internal incident reporting per clinic protocol (Exhibit A).
